The Domain Takeover Prevention Checklist Every Developer Needs

A practical security checklist to prevent subdomain takeover vulnerabilities — from proper CNAME validation to ensuring DNS records are deleted when deploying services.

As a developer, I've lost count of the number of times I've seen a simple oversight turn into a catastrophic security vulnerability. One of the most insidious and often-overlooked threats is the subdomain takeover, a type of attack where an attacker gains control of a subdomain by exploiting a misconfigured DNS setup. I recall a particularly harrowing experience where one of our developers at is-cool-me accidentally left a wildcard DNS record pointing to an unused Heroku instance. Before we knew it, an attacker had claimed the subdomain and was using it to phish our users. It was a sobering reminder of the importance of DNS safety and the need for a rigorous domain takeover prevention checklist. ## Understanding the Threat of Subdomain Takeover Subdomain takeover is a vulnerability that arises when a subdomain is no longer in use, but its DNS records still point to an external service, such as a Heroku or AWS instance. If an attacker can claim the subdomain by creating an account on the external service, they can then use it to host malicious content, such as phishing sites or malware. The threat is very real: according to a study by Detectify, over 10% of the top 100,000 websites have at least one vulnerable subdomain. To make matters worse, subdomain takeover can be incredibly difficult to detect, as the attacker's content may be hosted on a legitimate service, making it harder to distinguish from legitimate traffic. For example, if an attacker takes over the subdomain `blog.example.com` and uses it to host a phishing site, users may be tricked into entering their credentials, thinking they are on a legitimate site. ## Preventing Subdomain Takeover with DNS Safety So, how can you prevent subdomain takeover and ensure DNS safety? The first step is to regularly audit your DNS records and remove any unused subdomains. This can be a time-consuming process, but tools like DNSdumpster or SecurityTrails can help automate the task. Another crucial step is to use a canonical name (CNAME) record instead of an A record for subdomains. This ensures that if an attacker tries to claim a subdomain, they won't be able to use it to host malicious content. For instance, instead of using an A record to point `blog.example.com` to an IP address, use a CNAME record to point it to `example.herokuapp.com`. This way, even if an attacker claims the subdomain, they won't be able to use it to host content on Heroku. Additionally, consider implementing DNSSEC, which uses digital signatures to authenticate DNS records and prevent tampering. ## Implementing a Domain Takeover Prevention Checklist To ensure DNS safety and prevent subdomain takeover, it's essential to implement a rigorous domain takeover prevention checklist. Here are some key items to include: * Regularly audit DNS records to remove unused subdomains * Use CNAME records instead of A records for subdomains * Implement DNSSEC to authenticate DNS records * Monitor for suspicious activity on subdomains, such as unexpected changes to DNS records or sudden spikes in traffic * Use a tool like Subdomain Scanner or Sublist3r to identify vulnerable subdomains For example, at is-cool-me, we use a combination of DNSdumpster and SecurityTrails to monitor our DNS records and identify potential vulnerabilities. We also implement DNSSEC to authenticate our DNS records and prevent tampering. By following this checklist, you can significantly reduce the risk of subdomain takeover and ensure the security of your domain. ## Case Studies and War Stories I've seen firsthand the devastating consequences of subdomain takeover. In one case, a developer at a popular e-commerce site accidentally left a wildcard DNS record pointing to an unused AWS instance. Before long, an attacker had claimed the subdomain and was using it to host a phishing site, netting thousands of dollars in stolen credentials. The site's owners were left scrambling to respond to the attack, and their reputation suffered as a result. Another example is the case of GitHub, which was vulnerable to subdomain takeover due to a misconfigured DNS setup. An attacker was able to claim the subdomain `github.io` and use it to host a phishing site, putting thousands of users at risk. These cases highlight the importance of implementing a rigorous domain takeover prevention checklist and regularly auditing DNS records to prevent subdomain takeover. In conclusion, subdomain takeover is a serious security vulnerability that can have devastating consequences if left unchecked. By implementing a domain takeover prevention checklist and regularly auditing DNS records, you can significantly reduce the risk of subdomain takeover and ensure the security of your domain. Remember, DNS safety is not a one-time task, but an ongoing process that requires regular monitoring and maintenance. By following the checklist outlined above and staying vigilant, you can protect your users and your reputation from the threats of subdomain takeover. Key Takeaways: * Regularly audit DNS records to remove unused subdomains * Use CNAME records instead of A records for subdomains to prevent subdomain takeover * Implement DNSSEC to authenticate DNS records and prevent tampering * Monitor for suspicious activity on subdomains, such as unexpected changes to DNS records or sudden spikes in traffic Related Resources: * DNSdumpster: a tool for auditing DNS records and identifying potential vulnerabilities * SecurityTrails: a platform for monitoring DNS records and detecting suspicious activity * Subdomain Scanner: a tool for identifying vulnerable subdomains * Detectify: a security platform that offers subdomain takeover detection and prevention tools * OWASP: a resource for learning more about subdomain takeover and other security vulnerabilities
